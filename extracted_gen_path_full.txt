CHAPTER 1: CELLULAR REACTION TO INJURY
ðŸ”¬ OVERVIEW
Definition: Cellular reaction to injury encompasses all the morphologic and functional changes that cells undergo when exposed to stress or noxious stimuli.
Clinical Importance: Understanding cell injury is fundamental to pathology as it explains the basis of all disease processes. Recognizing reversible vs irreversible injury helps determine treatment windows.
I. ADAPTATION TO ENVIRONMENTAL STRESS
A. Hypertrophy
Increase in size of organ/tissue due to increase in cell SIZE (not number)
â†‘ Protein synthesis and â†‘ size/number of intracellular organelles
Examples: Skeletal muscle with exercise, Left ventricle in hypertension
KEY POINT: Result of increased WORKLOAD
B. Hyperplasia
Increase in organ/tissue size due to increase in cell NUMBER
Examples: Glandular proliferation in breast during pregnancy
Often occurs TOGETHER with hypertrophy (e.g., uterus in pregnancy)
KEY POINT: Only cells capable of division can undergo hyperplasia
C. Aplasia
FAILURE of cell production
Fetal development: Results in AGENESIS (absence of organ)
Later in life: Caused by permanent loss of precursor cells (e.g., bone marrow)
D. Hypoplasia
DECREASE in cell production (less extreme than aplasia)
Examples: Partial lack of gonadal growth in Turner syndrome, Klinefelter syndrome
E. Atrophy
Decrease in organ/tissue size due to decrease in mass of PREEXISTING cells
Causes: Disuse, nutritional/oxygen deprivation, â†“ endocrine stimulation, aging, denervation
Features: Autophagic granules (vacuoles with debris from degraded organelles)
Mechanism: Ubiquitin-proteasome pathway of protein degradation
F. Metaplasia
Definition: Replacement of one differentiated tissue by another
Types:
1. Squamous Metaplasia:
Columnar â†’ Squamous epithelium
Sites: Cervix (squamocolumnar junction), Bronchus, Endometrium, Pancreatic ducts
Causes: Chronic irritation (tobacco), Vitamin A deficiency
KEY POINT: Often REVERSIBLE
2. Osseous Metaplasia:
Formation of NEW BONE at sites of tissue injury
Cartilaginous metaplasia may also occur
3. Myeloid Metaplasia (Extramedullary Hematopoiesis):
Proliferation of hematopoietic tissue at sites OTHER than bone marrow
Sites: Liver, Spleen

III. FREE RADICAL INJURY
A. Definition
Free radicals: Chemical species with unpaired electrons that damage cells by lipid peroxidation, cross-linking proteins, and DNA fragmentation
B. Sources
Normal metabolism (oxidative phosphorylation)
Radiation
Inflammation (respiratory burst in neutrophils)
Chemicals and drugs
Reperfusion injury
C. Mechanisms of Damage
LIPID PEROXIDATION of cell membranes
CROSS-LINKING of proteins
FRAGMENTATION of DNA
D. Protection/Scavengers
SUPEROXIDE DISMUTASE (SOD): Converts superoxide to H2O2

GLUTATHIONE PEROXIDASE: Removes H2O2
VITAMINS E and C: Antioxidants
IV. CHEMICAL CELL INJURY
Direct toxicity to cellular components
Conversion to toxic metabolites (e.g., CCl4 â†’ CCl3 free radical)
Examples: Acetaminophen (liver), Carbon tetrachloride
V. NECROSIS
A. Definition
Necrosis: Morphologic changes that follow cell death in living tissue; involves enzymatic digestion of dead cells
B. Types of Necrosis
1. COAGULATIVE NECROSIS:
Most common type
Due to ISCHEMIA in most organs (EXCEPT brain)
Preservation of cell OUTLINE (ghost cells)
Increased eosinophilia (loss of RNA/DNA)
Examples: Myocardial infarction, Renal infarction
2. LIQUEFACTIVE NECROSIS:
Tissue transformed into viscous liquid
CNS ischemia (brain infarction)
Suppurative infections (abscess formation)
Due to rich lipid content and lack of structural framework
3. CASEOUS NECROSIS:
Cheese-like (caseous) appearance
Characteristic of TUBERCULOSIS
Amorphous, eosinophilic debris
Associated with granulomatous inflammation
4. FAT NECROSIS:
Specific to adipose tissue
Mechanisms:
   a) Enzymatic (pancreatitis) - lipase releases fatty acids â†’ calcium soaps (SAPONIFICATION)
   b) Traumatic (breast) - direct injury to fat
Chalky white appearance due to calcium deposits
Can be visualized on X-ray
5. FIBRINOID NECROSIS:
Immune-mediated vascular damage
Deposition of IMMUNE COMPLEXES and fibrin in vessel walls
Brightly eosinophilic ("fibrin-like")
Examples: Polyarteritis nodosa, Malignant hypertension
C. Nuclear Changes in Necrosis
PYKNOSIS: Nuclear shrinkage and increased basophilia
KARYORRHEXIS: Nuclear fragmentation
KARYOLYSIS: Fading of basophilia due to DNA digestion
ðŸ’¡ HIGH-YIELD: These nuclear changes are IRREVERSIBLE signs of cell death
VI. APOPTOSIS
A. Definition
Apoptosis: Programmed cell death - energy-dependent, genetically regulated process of cell suicide
B. Features (Contrast with Necrosis)
C. Physiologic Apoptosis
Organogenesis (embryonic development)
Hormone-dependent involution (menstrual cycle, lactation)
Deletion of autoreactive T cells in thymus
Cell turnover in proliferating tissues
D. Pathologic Apoptosis
DNA damage (p53-mediated)
Viral infections
Atrophy
Tumor cell death
E. Mechanisms
1. Intrinsic (Mitochondrial) Pathway:
Triggered by: DNA damage, growth factor deprivation, oxidative stress
Bcl-2 family proteins regulate mitochondrial permeability
Cytochrome c release â†’ caspase activation
p53 promotes apoptosis by transcription of bax gene
2. Extrinsic (Death Receptor) Pathway:
Triggered by Fas ligand and TNF
Fas-Fas ligand interaction important in immune regulation
Direct caspase activation
ðŸ’¡ HIGH-YIELD: Caspases are proteases that execute apoptosis. bcl-2 is anti-apoptotic; bax is pro-apoptotic
VII. REVERSIBLE CELLULAR CHANGES AND ACCUMULATIONS
A. Cellular Swelling (Hydropic Change)
First manifestation of cell injury
Due to failure of Na+-K+ pump â†’ water influx
Small clear vacuoles in cytoplasm
Reversible with restoration of blood flow
B. Fatty Change (Steatosis)
Accumulation of intracellular triglycerides
Most common in LIVER (also heart, kidney)
Causes: Alcoholism, Diabetes, Obesity, Protein malnutrition, Anoxia, Toxins
Mechanism: Imbalance between uptake, utilization, and mobilization of fat
Appearance: Clear vacuoles in cytoplasm (fat dissolves during processing)
Nucleus pushed to periphery (signet ring appearance)
C. Hyaline Change
Definition: Nonspecific term for any glassy, homogeneous, pink (eosinophilic) appearance
Examples:
   - Hyaline arteriolosclerosis (vessels in hypertension/diabetes)
   - Mallory bodies (alcoholic liver disease)
   - Russell bodies (plasma cells)
D. Pigment Accumulations
1. ENDOGENOUS PIGMENTS:
LIPOFUSIN ("wear-and-tear" pigment):
   - Brown, granular, perinuclear
   - Indigestible material from autophagocytosis
   - Associated with AGING and atrophy
   - Example: Brown atrophy of heart
MELANIN:
   - Black-brown pigment from tyrosine
   - Synthesized by melanocytes
   - Increased in melanocytic tumors
HEMOSIDERIN:
   - Golden-brown, granular
   - Iron storage form (ferritin aggregates)
   - Stains blue with Prussian blue
   - Local hemosiderosis: Local hemorrhage (bruise)
   - Systemic hemosiderosis: Excess body iron
   - HEMOCHROMATOSIS: Hereditary (HFE gene) or secondary iron overload
BILIRUBIN:
   - Yellow pigment from heme catabolism
   - JAUNDICE: Yellow discoloration from hyperbilirubinemia
   - Types: Hemolytic, Hepatocellular, Obstructive
2. EXOGENOUS PIGMENTS:
Carbon/anthracotic pigment (lungs - air pollution)
Tattoo pigments
Asbestos fibers
E. Pathologic Calcifications
1. DYSTROPHIC CALCIFICATION:
Calcium deposition in DAMAGED or DEAD tissues
NORMAL serum calcium levels
Examples: Atherosclerotic plaques, Old TB lesions, Fat necrosis
2. METASTATIC CALCIFICATION:
Calcium deposition in NORMAL tissues
ELEVATED serum calcium (hypercalcemia)
Causes of hypercalcemia:
   - Hyperparathyroidism
   - Malignancy with bone metastases
   - Vitamin D toxicity
   - Sarcoidosis
Sites: Kidney, Lungs, Stomach, Blood vessels

CHAPTER 2: INFLAMMATION
ðŸ”¬ OVERVIEW
Definition: Inflammation is a vascular response to injury involving exudation of fluid, attraction of leukocytes, and activation of chemical mediators.
Cardinal Signs (Celsus): Rubor (redness), Dolor (pain), Calor (heat), Tumor (swelling), Functio laesa (loss of function)
Causes: Infection, Trauma, Physical injury, Chemical injury, Immunologic injury, Tissue death
II. ACUTE INFLAMMATION
A. Adhesion Molecules
Role: Mediate leukocyte-endothelial interactions
Three Families:
SELECTINS:
   - E-selectin, P-selectin (endothelium)
   - L-selectin (leukocytes)
   - Bind to sialyl-Lewis X
   - Mediate ROLLING
IMMUNOGLOBULIN FAMILY:
   - ICAM-1, ICAM-2, VCAM
   - Bind to integrins
   - Mediate FIRM ADHESION
INTEGRINS:
   - LFA-1, MAC-1, VLA-4 (leukocytes)
   - Bind to Ig-family proteins
   - Mediate TRANSENDOTHELIAL MIGRATION
B. Vasoactive Changes
Brief vasoconstriction â†’ VASODILATION of arterioles, capillaries, venules
â†‘ Blood flow â†’ REDNESS (rubor) and HEAT (calor)
â†‘ Vascular permeability â†’ EDEMA (tumor)
Mechanisms: Endothelial cell contraction, Direct endothelial injury
C. Inflammatory Cells
NEUTROPHILS (First 24 hours):
   - Most prominent in early acute inflammation
   - Phagocytosis and killing of bacteria
   - NEUTROPHILIA in peripheral blood
   - BAND NEUTROPHILS (immature forms) indicate acute infection
MONOCYTES/MACROPHAGES (After 2-3 days):
   - Replace neutrophils
   - Longer lived, can proliferate in tissue
   - Phagocytosis of larger particles, debris
   - MONOCYTOSIS in TB, brucellosis, typhus
LYMPHOCYTES:
   - Prominent in VIRAL infections
   - Also in chronic inflammation
D. Leukocyte Events (The Migration Cascade)
1. MARGINATION: Leukocytes move to periphery of vessel
2. ROLLING: Selectin-mediated, reversible
3. ACTIVATION: Chemokines activate integrins on leukocytes
4. ADHESION: Integrin-mediated firm attachment
5. DIAPEDESIS: Migration through vessel wall (ICAM-1, PECAM-1)
6. CHEMOTAXIS: Directed movement toward chemoattractants
7. PHAGOCYTOSIS: Recognition, engulfment, killing
E. Chemotactic Factors
Bacterial products (N-formyl methionine peptides)
C5a (complement component)
Leukotriene B4 (LTB4)
Chemokines (IL-8)
F. Phagocytosis and Killing
RECOGNITION:
   - Opsonins enhance phagocytosis (IgG, C3b)

A. Characteristics

Cells: Macrophages, Lymphocytes, Plasma cells (NOT neutrophils)
Tissue destruction and repair occur simultaneously

B. Causes

IV. TISSUE REPAIR
A. Regeneration
Definition: Replacement of lost cells by proliferation of residual cells
Labile tissues: Continuously dividing (skin, gut, bone marrow)
Stable tissues: Can regenerate if stimulated (liver, kidney, pancreas)
Permanent tissues: Cannot regenerate (neurons, cardiac muscle, skeletal muscle)
B. Healing by Repair (Fibrosis)
Occurs when tissue cannot regenerate
Replacement by CONNECTIVE TISSUE (scar)
Steps:
   1. Formation of granulation tissue
   2. Collagen deposition
   3. Scar maturation
C. Granulation Tissue
Highly vascular, edematous, pink, granular appearance
Components:
   - New blood vessels (angiogenesis)
   - Fibroblasts
   - Inflammatory cells
   - Loose extracellular matrix
Functions: Fills defects, provides nutrients for healing
D. Wound Healing
PRIMARY UNION (First intention):
   - Clean, surgical incision with approximation
   - Minimal scarring
   - Steps: Clot formation â†’ Epidermal regeneration â†’ Dermal repair
SECONDARY UNION (Second intention):
   - Large wounds with tissue loss, infection
   - More extensive granulation tissue
   - Wound contraction (myofibroblasts)
   - Larger scar
E. Complications of Wound Healing
Infection
Wound dehiscence (separation)
Exuberant granulation (proud flesh)
Keloid formation (excessive collagen, especially in African Americans)
Contractures (limit function, especially over joints)

CHAPTER 3: HEMODYNAMIC DYSFUNCTION
ðŸ”¬ OVERVIEW
Definition: Hemodynamic dysfunction encompasses abnormalities in blood flow including hemorrhage, hyperemia, thrombosis, embolism, infarction, edema, and shock.
I. HEMORRHAGE
A. Definitions
HEMORRHAGE: Escape of blood from vasculature
HEMATOMA: Localized collection of blood in tissue
HEMOTHORAX: Blood in pleural cavity
HEMOPERICARDIUM: Blood in pericardial cavity
HEMARthROSIS: Blood in joint space
PETECHIAE: Small (1-2 mm) hemorrhages
PURPURA: Larger (3-5 mm) hemorrhages
ECCHYMOSES: Large (>1-2 cm) hemorrhages (bruises)
B. Causes
Vessel wall defect (trauma, inflammation)
Coagulation disorder
Platelet abnormality
II. HYPEREMIA
A. Active Hyperemia (Hyperemia)
Increased blood INFLOW due to arteriolar dilation
Physiologic: Exercise, blushing
Pathologic: Inflammation
B. Passive Hyperemia (Congestion)
Impaired blood OUTFLOW due to venous obstruction
ACUTE: Acute right heart failure â†’ acute hepatic congestion (nutmeg liver)
CHRONIC: Chronic right heart failure â†’ cardiac cirrhosis
Morphology:
   - Engorged, cyanotic tissue
   - Centrilobular necrosis (liver)
   - Hemosiderin-laden macrophages ("heart failure cells" in lungs)
III. INFARCTION
A. Definition
Infarction: Area of ischemic necrosis caused by occlusion of arterial supply or venous drainage
B. Types
COAGULATIVE infarct: Most organs (heart, kidney, spleen) - occlusion of arterial supply
LIQUEFACTIVE infarct: Brain - ischemia
HEMORRHAGIC infarct: Lung, intestine - dual circulation or loose tissue
C. Factors Influencing Infarct Development
Nature of vascular supply (end-arteries vs collateral circulation)
Rate of occlusion (slow allows collateral development)
Tissue vulnerability to hypoxia (neurons most sensitive)
Oxygen content of blood (anemia increases risk)
ðŸ’¡ HIGH-YIELD: Brain infarct = liquefactive; Heart infarct = coagulative; Lung infarct = hemorrhagic
IV. THROMBOSIS
A. Definitions
THROMBUS: Solid mass formed from blood constituents within vasculature during life
THROMBOSIS: Process of thrombus formation
POSTMORTEM CLOT: Forms after death (NOT a thrombus)
B. Virchow Triad (Risk Factors)
ENDOTHELIAL INJURY:
   - Most important factor
   - Atherosclerosis, trauma, inflammation
   - Exposes subendothelium (collagen, vWF)
STASIS OR TURBULENCE:
   - Atrial fibrillation, immobilization, varicose veins
   - Prevents washout of activated clotting factors
HYPERCOAGULABILITY:
   - Primary (genetic): Factor V Leiden, Prothrombin G20210A, Antithrombin III deficiency
   - Secondary (acquired): Malignancy, pregnancy, OCPs, smoking
C. Thrombus Formation
1. Platelet adhesion: vWF binds to exposed collagen â†’ platelet glycoprotein Ib
2. Platelet activation: Shape change, granule release (ADP, TXA2)
3. Platelet aggregation: GP IIb-IIIa links platelets via fibrinogen
4. Coagulation cascade activation: Thrombin generation
5. Fibrin formation: Stabilizes platelet plug
D. Lines of Zahn
Alternating pale (platelets/fibrin) and dark (RBCs) bands in thrombus
Significance: Indicates thrombus formed in flowing blood (vs postmortem clot)
E. Fate of Thrombus
DISSOLUTION: Fibrinolysis by plasmin
ORGANIZATION: Ingrowth of smooth muscle and fibroblasts â†’ recanalization
CALCIFICATION: Dystrophic calcification
EMBOLIZATION: Thrombus breaks off and travels
F. Clinical Consequences
Occlusion of vessel â†’ ischemia/infarction
Embolization
Thrombosis on cardiac valves (vegetations)
