2) CORE DEFINITIONS (Exam Wording)

Epidemiology

  • Definition 1: Study of distribution and determinants of health-related states in specified populations
  • Definition 2: Application of this study to control health problems
  • Key term: 'Distribution' = Person, Place, Time patterns
  • Key term: 'Determinants' = Factors that influence health outcomes

Epidemiologic Triad

  • Classic model explaining disease occurrence through interaction of three components
  • Agent = Factor whose presence causes disease (bacteria, virus, chemical)
  • Host = Person or population susceptible to disease
  • Environment = External conditions that allow disease transmission

3) CONCEPT BUILD

Aims of Epidemiology

  • 1. DESCRIBE disease distribution: Who (person), Where (place), When (time)
  •    Dental Example: Caries more common in lower SES children (person), urban areas (place), after sugar exposure (time)
  • 2. EXPLAIN disease etiology: Identify causes and risk factors
  •    Dental Example: Streptococcus mutans + sucrose = caries initiation
  • 3. PREDICT disease occurrence: Estimate future cases for planning
  •    Dental Example: Projecting oral cancer cases based on tobacco use trends
  • 4. CONTROL disease: Develop and evaluate prevention strategies
  •    Dental Example: Water fluoridation reduces caries by 40-60%

Epidemiologic Triad - Dental Examples

  • DENTAL CARIES:
  •   Agent: Streptococcus mutans, Lactobacillus
  •   Host: Tooth morphology, saliva flow, immune status
  •   Environment: Diet (sugar frequency), fluoride exposure, oral hygiene

  • PERIODONTAL DISEASE:
  •   Agent: Porphyromonas gingivalis, Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans
  •   Host: Genetic susceptibility, diabetes, immune response
  •   Environment: Smoking, stress, oral hygiene practices

  • ORAL CANCER:
  •   Agent: Tobacco carcinogens, HPV (types 16, 18)
  •   Host: Genetic predisposition, nutritional status (iron deficiency)
  •   Environment: Tobacco use, alcohol consumption, betel nut chewing

4) MEASUREMENTS IN EPIDEMIOLOGY

Rates, Ratios, Proportions

  • RATE: Measures occurrence of event in population over time
  •   Formula: (Number of events / Population at risk) x Time multiplier
  •   Example: Caries incidence = 2 new lesions per 100 children per year

  • RATIO: Comparison of two quantities (numerator not in denominator)
  •   Formula: A / B (no time component)
  •   Example: Dentist-to-population ratio = 1:5000

  • PROPORTION: Numerator is subset of denominator (no time)
  •   Formula: (Part / Whole) x 100
  •   Example: 60% of adults have periodontal disease

Incidence

  • CUMULATIVE INCIDENCE (CI): Proportion developing disease over time period
  •   Formula: CI = New cases / Population at risk at start
  •   Example: 10 new caries cases among 100 children over 1 year = 10%

  • INCIDENCE RATE (IR): Rate per person-time of observation
  •   Formula: IR = New cases / Total person-time at risk
  •   Example: 5 cases per 1000 person-years

  • INTERPRETATION:
  •   High incidence = Active disease transmission/risk
  •   Low incidence = Effective prevention/control measures working

Prevalence

  • POINT PREVALENCE: Proportion with disease at specific point in time
  •   Formula: Cases at time T / Population at time T
  •   Example: 30% of schoolchildren have dental caries on Jan 1, 2024

  • PERIOD PREVALENCE: Proportion with disease during a time period
  •   Formula: Cases during period / Average population during period
  •   Example: 45% of adults visited dentist in past 12 months

  • INTERPRETATION:
  •   High prevalence = Large disease burden (plan services)
  •   Low prevalence = Disease well-controlled or short duration

Relationship: Incidence vs Prevalence

  • Prevalence = Incidence x Average Duration of disease

  • Factors INCREASING Prevalence:
  •   - Longer disease duration (chronic conditions)
  •   - Improved treatment (people live longer with disease)
  •   - Increased incidence

  • Factors DECREASING Prevalence:
  •   - Shorter disease duration (effective cure)
  •   - Decreased incidence
  •   - High mortality from disease

Relative Risk (RR)

  • DEFINITION: Ratio of disease incidence in exposed vs unexposed groups
  • FORMULA: RR = Incidence in exposed / Incidence in unexposed = (a/a+b) / (c/c+d)

  • INTERPRETATION:
  •   RR = 1: No association between exposure and disease
  •   RR > 1: Increased risk (positive association)
  •   RR < 1: Decreased risk (protective factor)

  • DENTAL EXAMPLE:
  •   Smokers: 30% periodontal disease
  •   Non-smokers: 10% periodontal disease
  •   RR = 30/10 = 3.0 (Smokers have 3x higher risk)

  • WHEN USED: Cohort studies (prospective/retrospective)

Odds Ratio (OR)

  • DEFINITION: Ratio of odds of exposure in cases vs controls
  • FORMULA: OR = (a x d) / (b x c) where a=exposed cases, b=exposed controls, etc.

  • INTERPRETATION:
  •   OR = 1: No association
  •   OR > 1: Exposure increases odds of disease
  •   OR < 1: Exposure decreases odds of disease (protective)

  • DENTAL EXAMPLE (Case-Control):
  •   Oral cancer cases: 80 smokers, 20 non-smokers
  •   Controls: 40 smokers, 60 non-smokers
  •   OR = (80 x 60) / (20 x 40) = 4800/800 = 6.0
  •   Interpretation: Smokers have 6x higher odds of oral cancer

  • WHEN USED: Case-control studies (when incidence cannot be calculated)

Attributable Risk (AR)

  • DEFINITION: Excess incidence in exposed group attributable to exposure
  • FORMULA: AR = Incidence exposed - Incidence unexposed

  • POPULATION ATTRIBUTABLE RISK (PAR):
  •   Excess incidence in total population due to exposure
  •   PAR = Incidence total - Incidence unexposed

  • USE: Public health planning - how much disease can be prevented
  • DENTAL EXAMPLE: If smoking cessation reduces periodontal disease by AR amount

Bias vs Confounding

  • BIAS: Systematic error in study design/conduct leading to wrong results
  •   Types: Selection bias, Information bias (recall, observer), Publication bias
  •   DENTAL EXAMPLE: Recall bias - caries patients may recall sugar intake differently

  • CONFOUNDING: Distortion of exposure-disease relationship by third variable
  •   Confounder associated with BOTH exposure AND disease
  •   DENTAL EXAMPLE: Socioeconomic status confounds sugar-caries relationship
  •   Control methods: Randomization, Restriction, Matching, Stratification

5) EPIDEMIOLOGIC STUDY DESIGNS

Descriptive Epidemiology

  • PURPOSE: Describe disease patterns by Person, Place, Time
  • PERSON: Age, sex, race, occupation, socioeconomic status
  • PLACE: Geographic variation, urban-rural differences
  • TIME: Secular trends, seasonal patterns, cyclic changes

  • DENTAL EXAMPLES:
  •   Person: Caries higher in children, periodontal disease higher in adults
  •   Place: Fluorosis endemic in high-fluoride areas
  •   Time: Seasonal variation in dental trauma (summer sports)

Analytical Epidemiology - Case-Control Study

  • DESIGN: Start with outcome (disease), look back at exposure
  • DIRECTION: Retrospective (outcome -> exposure)
  • MEASURE: Odds Ratio (OR)

  • STRENGTHS:
  •   - Good for rare diseases
  •   - Relatively quick and inexpensive
  •   - Can study multiple exposures

  • LIMITATIONS:
  •   - Cannot calculate incidence/risk directly
  •   - Recall bias (cases may remember exposures differently)
  •   - Temporal ambiguity (did exposure precede disease?)

  • DENTAL EXAMPLE: Oral cancer cases vs controls, assess tobacco/alcohol history

Analytical Epidemiology - Cohort Study

  • DESIGN: Start with exposure, follow forward for outcome
  • DIRECTION: Prospective (exposure -> outcome) or Retrospective
  • MEASURE: Relative Risk (RR), Attributable Risk (AR)

  • STRENGTHS:
  •   - Can calculate incidence and risk directly
  •   - Clear temporal sequence
  •   - Good for multiple outcomes from one exposure

  • LIMITATIONS:
  •   - Expensive and time-consuming
  •   - Loss to follow-up
  •   - Not suitable for rare diseases

  • DENTAL EXAMPLE: Follow smokers vs non-smokers for 10 years, measure periodontal disease

Experimental Epidemiology

  • RANDOMIZED CONTROLLED TRIAL (RCT):
  •   - Gold standard for intervention evaluation
  •   - Random allocation to treatment/control groups
  •   - Double-blinding: Neither participant nor investigator knows allocation
  •   - Allocation concealment: Randomization sequence hidden until assignment

  • FIELD TRIAL: Intervention in healthy individuals (primary prevention)
  •   DENTAL EXAMPLE: Fluoride varnish application to prevent caries

  • COMMUNITY TRIAL: Intervention at community level
  •   DENTAL EXAMPLE: Water fluoridation in entire community

6) INVESTIGATION OF AN EPIDEMIC

Steps in Epidemic Investigation (Flowchart)

  • STEP 1: VERIFY THE EPIDEMIC
  •   Compare current cases with baseline/previous data
  •   Rule out artificial increase (improved reporting, diagnostic change)

  • STEP 2: CONFIRM DIAGNOSIS
  •   Clinical examination, laboratory confirmation
  •   Ensure case definition is consistent

  • STEP 3: DEFINE CASE AND COUNT CASES
  •   Establish case definition (clinical + lab + epidemiologic criteria)
  •   Active case finding in community

  • STEP 4: ORIENT DATA (Person, Place, Time)
  •   Who is affected? Where? When did it start?

  • STEP 5: CONSTRUCT EPIDEMIC CURVE
  •   Histogram: Cases vs Time of onset
  •   Shape suggests mode of transmission

  • STEP 6: FORMULATE AND TEST HYPOTHESIS
  •   Common source vs propagated epidemic
  •   Analytic studies to test hypotheses

  • STEP 7: IMPLEMENT CONTROL MEASURES
  •   Treat cases, protect susceptible, interrupt transmission

  • STEP 8: REPORT AND FOLLOW-UP
  •   Document findings, recommendations, monitor effectiveness

Epidemic Curve Interpretation

  • COMMON SOURCE (Point Source):
  •   Shape: Sharp upward slope, single peak, gradual decline
  •   Example: Foodborne outbreak from contaminated food at single event

  • COMMON SOURCE (Continuous):
  •   Shape: Plateau instead of peak
  •   Example: Water supply contamination over weeks

  • PROPAGATED (Person-to-Person):
  •   Shape: Series of progressively taller peaks (waves)
  •   Example: Influenza, measles transmission
  •   Incubation period visible between peaks

7) KEY TERMINOLOGIES

  • ENDEMIC: Constant presence of disease in population (baseline level)
  • EPIDEMIC: Occurrence exceeding normal endemic level
  • PANDEMIC: Epidemic spreading across countries/continents
  • SPORADIC: Occasional cases, no pattern
  • OUTBREAK: Localized epidemic (same as epidemic, smaller scale)

  • INDEX CASE: First case identified in outbreak
  • SECONDARY CASE: Cases infected by index case
  • ATTACK RATE: Incidence during epidemic (usually expressed as %)
  • CASE FATALITY RATE (CFR): Proportion of cases that die from disease

Sensitivity, Specificity, PPV, NPV

  • SENSITIVITY: Ability to correctly identify those WITH disease
  •   Formula: True Positives / (True Positives + False Negatives)
  •   'Rule OUT' disease - high sensitivity misses few cases

  • SPECIFICITY: Ability to correctly identify those WITHOUT disease
  •   Formula: True Negatives / (True Negatives + False Positives)
  •   'Rule IN' disease - high specificity has few false alarms

  • POSITIVE PREDICTIVE VALUE (PPV): Probability disease present given positive test
  • NEGATIVE PREDICTIVE VALUE (NPV): Probability disease absent given negative test

9) HIGH-YIELD EXAM POINTS

  • Epidemiology = Study of distribution AND determinants
  • Triad components must ALL be present for disease occurrence
  • Incidence measures NEW cases; Prevalence measures ALL existing cases
  • Prevalence = Incidence x Duration

  • OR approximates RR when disease is RARE (<10%)
  • Confounder = Associated with BOTH exposure AND outcome
  • Randomization eliminates confounding AND selection bias

  • Sensitivity = True Positive Rate; Specificity = True Negative Rate
  • Epidemic curve shape reveals transmission pattern
  • Attack rate used during epidemics (expressed as percentage)

  • Sensitivity: Test's ability to detect true positives

 CHAPTER 8: EPIDEMIOLOGY OF ORAL DISEASES

DENTAL CARIES

1) Definition (BDS Exam Wording)

  • Definition 1: Localized, post-eruptive, pathological destruction of tooth tissue
  • Definition 2: Multifactorial, transmissible, infectious oral disease resulting in dissolution of enamel and dentin
  • Key terms: 'Post-eruptive' (after tooth erupts), 'Localized' (specific sites)
  • Key terms: 'Multifactorial' (many causes), 'Transmissible' (bacteria can spread)

2) Epidemiology (Person/Place/Time)

  • PERSON:
  •   - Age: Peak in children 5-7 years (primary teeth), 11-14 years (permanent teeth)
  •   - Sex: Historically higher in females (more teeth, better dental attendance)
  •   - SES: Inverse relationship - higher in lower socioeconomic groups
  •   - Race: Varies by geographic region and dietary habits

  • PLACE:
  •   - Higher in developed countries (more sugar consumption)
  •   - Lower in fluoride-rich areas
  •   - Urban > Rural (dietary differences)

  • TIME:
  •   - Declining trend in developed countries (fluoride, awareness)
  •   - Increasing in developing countries (westernized diet)

3) Etiology & Risk Factors

  • NON-MODIFIABLE:
  •   - Age: Young children and elderly at higher risk
  •   - Genetics: Enamel structure, salivary composition
  •   - Tooth morphology: Deep pits and fissures

  • MODIFIABLE:
  •   - Diet: Frequency of sugar intake > Amount of sugar
  •   - Oral hygiene: Plaque accumulation
  •   - Fluoride exposure: Protective factor
  •   - Salivary flow: Xerostomia increases risk

  • BEHAVIORAL:
  •   - Sugar snacking frequency
  •   - Irregular brushing habits
  •   - Irregular dental visits

  • MICROBIAL:
  •   - Streptococcus mutans: Primary initiator
  •   - Lactobacillus: Progression of lesions
  •   - Candida: Root caries association

  • DIETARY:
  •   - Sucrose (most cariogenic)
  •   - Sticky foods (longer oral retention)
  •   - Frequency > Quantity (Stephan curve)

4) Pathogenesis Overview

  • STEP 1: Bacterial adhesion to tooth surface (pellicle formation)
  • STEP 2: Plaque biofilm maturation (S.mutans colonization)
  • STEP 3: Sugar fermentation -> Acid production (pH drops below 5.5)
  • STEP 4: Demineralization of enamel (calcium/phosphate loss)
  • STEP 5: Remineralization (if pH rises) OR Cavity formation (if demineralization continues)

  • CRITICAL pH: 5.5 (enamel dissolution begins)
  • STEPHAN CURVE: pH drops after sugar intake, recovers in 30-60 minutes

5) Levels of Prevention

  • PRIMORDIAL: Prevent risk factors from occurring
  •   Example: National dietary guidelines limiting sugar

  • PRIMARY: Prevent disease onset
  •   - Fluoride: Water, toothpaste, varnish, gel
  •   - Sealants: Pit and fissure sealants
  •   - Diet counseling: Reduce sugar frequency
  •   - Oral hygiene instruction

  • SECONDARY: Early detection and prompt treatment
  •   - Regular dental check-ups
  •   - Radiographic screening
  •   - Minimal intervention: Remineralization, atraumatic restorative treatment

  • TERTIARY: Restore function, prevent complications
  •   - Fillings, crowns, root canal treatment
  •   - Extractions and prosthetic rehabilitation

6) Screening / Early Detection

  • WHAT TO SCREEN: White spot lesions (reversible), early cavitations
  • WHERE: Schools, community centers, dental clinics
  • HOW: Visual-tactile examination, radiographs (bitewing for interproximal)

  • WARNINGS:
  •   - False positives: Over-diagnosis leading to unnecessary treatment
  •   - False negatives: Missed lesions requiring more complex treatment later

7) Caries Risk Concept

  • HIGH RISK INDICATORS:
  •   - Active caries in past 12 months
  •   - Visible plaque on lower anterior teeth
  •   - Frequent sugar intake between meals
  •   - Low fluoride exposure
  •   - Xerostomia (medication-induced or disease-related)

  • PROTECTIVE FACTORS:
  •   - Adequate saliva flow and buffering capacity
  •   - Regular fluoride exposure
  •   - Good oral hygiene practices
  •   - Regular dental attendance

ORAL CANCER

1) Definition

  • Definition: Malignant neoplasm arising from the oral cavity epithelium
  • Includes: Lip, buccal mucosa, gingiva, retromolar area, floor of mouth, palate, tonsil, tongue
  • Most common type: Squamous cell carcinoma (90%+ of oral cancers)

2) Epidemiology

  • PERSON:
  •   - Age: Peak 50-70 years (rare <40 years)
  •   - Sex: Male:Female ratio 2:1 to 4:1 (changing due to female tobacco use)
  •   - SES: Higher in lower socioeconomic groups

  • PLACE:
  •   - Highest in South and Southeast Asia (India, Sri Lanka, Pakistan)
  •   - High in Eastern Europe, parts of South America
  •   - Lower in Western Europe, North America

  • TIME:
  •   - Increasing in younger populations
  •   - HPV-related oropharyngeal cancer increasing in developed countries

3) Etiology & Risk Factors

  • TOBACCO (Most important):
  •   - Smoking: Cigarettes, bidis, cigars
  •   - Smokeless: Gutka, khaini, paan masala, snuff
  •   - Synergistic effect with alcohol

  • ALCOHOL:
  •   - Independent risk factor
  •   - Multiplicative effect with tobacco

  • BETEL QUID / PAAN:
  •   - Common in South Asia
  •   - Contains areca nut (carcinogenic)

  • HPV INFECTION:
  •   - HPV 16 and 18 (high-risk types)
  •   - Associated with oropharyngeal cancer

  • OTHER FACTORS:
  •   - Nutritional deficiency (iron, folate, vitamin A)
  •   - Immunosuppression
  •   - Sharp tooth surfaces/ill-fitting dentures (chronic irritation)

4) Common Sites

  • Tongue (most common, especially lateral border)
  • Floor of mouth
  • Buccal mucosa (betel quid chewers)
  • Retromolar area
  • Soft palate and tonsillar pillars
  • Lower lip (outdoor workers - sun exposure)
  • Gingiva and alveolar ridge

5) Warning Signs (Red Flags)

  • Non-healing ulcer >2-3 weeks
  • Red (erythroplakia) or white (leukoplakia) patches
  • Lump or thickening in oral soft tissues
  • Difficulty chewing, swallowing, or speaking
  • Persistent sore throat or hoarseness
  • Numbness in oral/facial region
  • Unexplained tooth mobility
  • Ear pain (referred from tongue/base of tongue)

6) Potentially Malignant Disorders

  • LEUKOPLAKIA: White patch that cannot be scraped off or diagnosed as any other disease
  •   - Malignant transformation: 1-5% over 10 years

  • ERYTHROPLAKIA: Red patch with higher malignant potential
  •   - Malignant transformation: Up to 50%

  • ORAL SUBMUCOUS FIBROSIS: Fibrosis of submucosal tissues
  •   - Associated with areca nut chewing
  •   - Trismus (inability to open mouth)

  • LICHEN PLANUS: Especially erosive type
  •   - Low malignant potential but requires monitoring

7) Levels of Prevention

  • PRIMARY:
  •   - Tobacco cessation programs
  •   - Alcohol moderation counseling
  •   - HPV vaccination (for oropharyngeal cancer prevention)
  •   - Dietary counseling (antioxidant-rich foods)

  • SECONDARY:
  •   - Screening high-risk populations
  •   - Visual oral examination (VOE)
  •   - Toluidine blue staining for suspicious lesions
  •   - Biopsy of persistent lesions

  • TERTIARY:
  •   - Surgery, radiotherapy, chemotherapy
  •   - Rehabilitation and palliative care

8) Viva One-Liners

  • Oral cancer = 6th most common cancer globally
  • Tobacco + Alcohol = Multiplicative (not additive) risk
  • Tongue is the most common site for oral cancer
  • Erythroplakia has higher malignant potential than leukoplakia
  • HPV 16 is most associated with oropharyngeal cancer
  • South Asia has the highest oral cancer rates globally

PERIODONTAL DISEASES

1) Definition

  • Definition: Inflammatory diseases of the supporting tissues of teeth
  • Includes: Gingivitis (reversible) and Periodontitis (irreversible)
  • Key feature: Destruction of periodontal ligament and alveolar bone

2) Epidemiology

  • PERSON:
  •   - Age: Gingivitis in children; Periodontitis increases with age
  •   - Sex: Males > Females (behavioral differences)
  •   - SES: Lower SES associated with more severe disease

  • PLACE:
  •   - Worldwide prevalence: 20-50% for severe periodontitis
  •   - Higher in developing countries

  • TIME:
  •   - Chronic, slowly progressing disease
  •   - Onset typically in adulthood

3) Etiology & Risk Factors

  • PRIMARY ETIOLOGY: Dental plaque (bacterial biofilm)

  • KEY BACTERIA:
  •   - Porphyromonas gingivalis
  •   - Tannerella forsythia
  •   - Treponema denticola
  •   - Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans (aggressive periodontitis)

  • MODIFIABLE RISK FACTORS:
  •   - SMOKING: Strongest modifiable risk factor (2-7x increased risk)
  •   - DIABETES: Poor glycemic control increases risk and severity
  •   - ORAL HYGIENE: Poor plaque control
  •   - OBESITY: Associated with increased inflammation

  • NON-MODIFIABLE RISK FACTORS:
  •   - GENETICS: Family aggregation observed
  •   - AGE: Cumulative exposure to risk factors
  •   - RACE: Some populations show higher susceptibility

  • OTHER FACTORS:
  •   - Stress (impaired immune response)
  •   - Medications (phenytoin, cyclosporine, nifedipine - gingival overgrowth)
  •   - Hormonal changes (pregnancy, puberty)

4) Pathogenesis Overview

  • PHASE 1: Bacterial colonization (dental plaque accumulation)
  • PHASE 2: Host inflammatory response (acute inflammation)
  • PHASE 3: Chronic inflammation (gingivitis - reversible)
  • PHASE 4: Tissue destruction (periodontitis - irreversible)
  •   - Collagen breakdown
  •   - Periodontal ligament destruction
  •   - Alveolar bone resorption
  • PHASE 5: Pocket formation and tooth mobility

5) Levels of Prevention

  • PRIMARY:
  •   - Plaque control: Tooth brushing, interdental cleaning
  •   - Smoking cessation
  •   - Diabetes management
  •   - Professional prophylaxis

  • SECONDARY:
  •   - Early detection through periodontal screening
  •   - Scaling and root planing
  •   - Local antimicrobial therapy

  • TERTIARY:
  •   - Periodontal surgery
  •   - Regenerative procedures
  •   - Maintenance therapy

6) Community Control Measures

  • CPITN/CPI screening for community assessment
  • Oral health education in schools and workplaces
  • Integration with diabetes screening programs
  • Tobacco cessation support services

MALOCCLUSION

1) Definition

  • Definition: Any deviation from normal occlusion (ideal tooth relationship)
  • WHO Definition: Significant deviation from normal occlusion affecting function and aesthetics
  • Note: Not a disease but a variation from ideal

2) Epidemiology

  • PERSON:
  •   - Age: Detected in childhood, treatment in adolescence
  •   - No significant sex difference

  • PLACE:
  •   - Higher in developed countries (dietary differences - softer foods)
  •   - Varies by ethnicity

  • PREVALENCE:
  •   - 30-50% have some form of malocclusion
  •   - Only 10-15% have severe malocclusion requiring treatment

3) Determinants

  • GENETIC FACTORS:
  •   - Jaw size discrepancies
  •   - Tooth size-jaw size discrepancy
  •   - Inherited traits (protrusion, crowding)

  • ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS:
  •   - Thumb sucking (anterior open bite)
  •   - Mouth breathing (adenoid facies)
  •   - Early loss of primary teeth (space loss)
  •   - Trauma
  •   - Dietary consistency (soft diet = reduced jaw development)

4) Classification Overview

  • ANGLE'S CLASSIFICATION:
  •   - Class I: Normal molar relationship with crowding/spacing
  •   - Class II: Mandible retruded (Division 1: proclined incisors; Division 2: retroclined incisors)
  •   - Class III: Mandible protruded (underbite)

  • DENTAL HEALTH COMPONENT (IOTN):
  •   - Grade 1-4: No treatment need
  •   - Grade 5-7: Moderate need
  •   - Grade 8-10: Great need

5) Prevention

  • PRIMORDIAL: Breastfeeding, appropriate weaning

  • PRIMARY:
  •   - Discourage thumb sucking, pacifier use beyond age 3
  •   - Treat mouth breathing (ENT referral)
  •   - Maintain primary teeth (space maintainers if lost early)

  • SECONDARY (INTERCEPTION):
  •   - Early orthodontic evaluation (age 7)
  •   - Space regainers
  •   - Habit-breaking appliances

  • TERTIARY:
  •   - Comprehensive orthodontic treatment
  •   - Orthognathic surgery

DENTAL FLUOROSIS

1) Definition

  • Definition: Hypomineralization of enamel due to excessive fluoride intake during tooth development
  • Alternative: Mottled enamel
  • Critical period: Prenatal to 8 years (enamel formation)

2) Mechanism

  • Excess fluoride affects ameloblast function
  • Results in porous enamel with increased protein content
  • Enamel is softer and more porous than normal
  • Stains more easily due to porosity

3) Sources of Fluoride

  • WATER: Natural or fluoridated (optimal 0.7-1.2 ppm)
  • TOOTHPASTE: 1000-1500 ppm fluoride
  • DIETARY SUPPLEMENTS: Tablets, drops
  • PROFESSIONAL APPLICATIONS: Gels, varnishes, foams
  • FOOD: Tea, fish, processed foods made with fluoridated water

4) Dean's Fluorosis Index (Clinical Grading)

  • NORMAL (0): Enamel smooth, glossy, translucent, pale cream color

  • QUESTIONABLE (0.5): Small opaque areas covering <25% of tooth surface

  • VERY MILD (1): Small opaque areas covering 25-50% of tooth surface

  • MILD (2): Opaque areas covering >50% of tooth surface
  •   - No brown stains, no pitting

  • MODERATE (3): All enamel surfaces affected
  •   - Brown stains present
  •   - Some pitting may be present

  • SEVERE (4): All enamel surfaces affected
  •   - Discrete pitting present
  •   - Brown stains, tooth form may be affected

5) Prevention

  • COMMUNITY:
  •   - Monitor water fluoride levels
  •   - Defluoridation in high-fluoride areas (>2 ppm)
  •   - Education about fluoride sources

  • CLINIC:
  •   - Supervise toothpaste use in children (<pea-sized amount)
  •   - Avoid fluoride supplements in high-fluoride areas
  •   - Use low-fluoride toothpaste for young children

6) MCQ Traps for Oral Diseases

  • TRAP: Caries is NOT caused by sugar AMOUNT but by sugar FREQUENCY
  • TRAP: Fluorosis occurs during TOOTH DEVELOPMENT, not after eruption
  • TRAP: Periodontitis is IRREVERSIBLE; gingivitis is REVERSIBLE
  • TRAP: Smoking is the STRONGEST modifiable risk factor for periodontitis
  • TRAP: Tongue (not buccal mucosa) is most common site for oral cancer
  • TRAP: Erythroplakia has HIGHER malignant potential than leukoplakia
  • TRAP: Critical pH for enamel is 5.5 (not 7.0)
  • TRAP: S.mutans is the PRIMARY initiator of caries

 CHAPTER 9: INFECTION CONTROL AND STERILIZATION

1) EXAM MAP

  • Long Questions: Chain of infection, Sterilization methods, Standard precautions
  • Short Notes: Autoclave, Chemical sterilants, PPE, Hand hygiene
  • Viva: Difference between sterilization and disinfection, Spaulding classification

2) CHAIN OF INFECTION

  • INFECTIOUS AGENT: Bacteria, viruses, fungi, prions
  •   Dental examples: Hepatitis B, HIV, Mycobacterium tuberculosis, SARS-CoV-2

  • RESERVOIR: Where agent lives and multiplies
  •   Dental examples: Patient's blood/saliva, contaminated instruments

  • PORTAL OF EXIT: How agent leaves reservoir
  •   Dental examples: Blood, saliva, respiratory droplets

  • MODE OF TRANSMISSION: How agent spreads
  •   Contact: Direct (patient to staff) or Indirect (contaminated surfaces)
  •   Droplet: Coughing, sneezing, talking (within 1 meter)
  •   Airborne: Small particles that remain suspended (TB, measles, chickenpox)

  • PORTAL OF ENTRY: How agent enters new host
  •   Dental examples: Needlestick injuries, mucous membrane exposure, inhalation

  • SUSCEPTIBLE HOST: Person at risk of infection
  •   Factors: Immune status, vaccination, age, underlying disease

3) STANDARD PRECAUTIONS

  • DEFINITION: Minimum infection control practices for ALL patients, ALL procedures
  • Rationale: Treat all blood and body fluids as potentially infectious

  • COMPONENTS:
  •   1. HAND HYGIENE: Most important infection control measure
  •      - When: Before/after patient contact, before aseptic procedures, after body fluid exposure
  •      - Methods: Soap and water (20 seconds) or Alcohol-based hand rub (20-30 seconds)
  •      - Use soap and water when hands visibly soiled or after restroom

  •   2. SHARPS SAFETY:
  •      - Never recap needles using both hands
  •      - Use safety-engineered devices
  •      - Dispose immediately in puncture-resistant sharps container
  •      - Fill container only 2/3 full

  •   3. PERSONAL PROTECTIVE EQUIPMENT (PPE):
  •      - Gloves: For all procedures involving blood/saliva
  •      - Masks: Protect against splatter and aerosols
  •      - Protective eyewear: For all dental procedures
  •      - Face shields: Additional protection for high-risk procedures
  •      - Gowns: Fluid-resistant for procedures with splash risk

  •   4. RESPIRATORY HYGIENE/COUGH ETIQUETTE:
  •      - Cover mouth/nose when coughing
  •      - Use tissues and dispose immediately
  •      - Hand hygiene after contact with respiratory secretions

4) AIRBORNE INFECTION (Dental Aerosol Context)

  • AEROSOLS vs SPLATTER:
  •   Aerosols: Particles <50 micrometers, remain airborne, travel long distances
  •   Splatter: Larger droplets (>50 micrometers), fall quickly within 1 meter

  • RISK REDUCTION MEASURES:
  •   1. VENTILATION: Adequate air exchanges (6-12 per hour)
  •   2. HIGH-VOLUME EVACUATION (HVE): Reduces aerosols by 90%+
  •   3. RUBBER DAM: Reduces aerosols by 70%+
  •   4. PRE-PROCEDURAL MOUTHRINSE: Reduces bacterial load (chlorhexidine, essential oils)
  •   5. PROTECTIVE EQUIPMENT: N95/FFP2 masks for high-risk procedures

5) STERILIZATION vs DISINFECTION vs ANTISEPSIS

  • STERILIZATION: Destruction of ALL microbial life including spores
  •   Methods: Steam autoclave, dry heat, chemical sterilants
  •   Use: Surgical instruments, critical items

  • DISINFECTION: Destruction of MOST pathogenic organisms (NOT spores)
  •   Levels: High, Intermediate, Low
  •   Use: Environmental surfaces, non-critical items

  • ANTISEPSIS: Chemical inhibition of microorganisms on LIVING TISSUE
  •   Examples: Alcohol hand rub, povidone-iodine on skin
  •   Use: Skin preparation, wound cleaning

6) STERILIZATION METHODS

Steam Autoclave (Most Common)

  • PRINCIPLE: Moist heat denatures proteins and enzymes
  • PARAMETERS:
  •   - Temperature: 121C (250F) at 15 psi for 15-30 minutes
  •   - Temperature: 134C (273F) at 30 psi for 3-10 minutes (prevacuum)

  • MONITORING:
  •   - Physical: Temperature, pressure, time gauges
  •   - Chemical: Indicator strips/tapes change color
  •   - Biological: Spore tests (Geobacillus stearothermophilus) weekly

Dry Heat

  • PRINCIPLE: Oxidation and protein denaturation
  • PARAMETERS: 160-170C for 1-2 hours
  • USES: Items damaged by moisture (oils, powders, sharp instruments)
  • LIMITATION: Longer time required, higher temperatures

Chemical Sterilants

  • GLUTARALDEHYDE (2%): 10 hours for sterilization
  •   - Toxic, requires ventilation
  •   - Use: Heat-sensitive instruments (endoscopes)

  • HYDROGEN PEROXIDE GAS PLASMA: Low temperature
  •   - Use: Heat and moisture-sensitive equipment

  • PERACETIC ACID: Rapid action, environmentally friendly

7) INSTRUMENT PROCESSING WORKFLOW

  • STEP 1: CLEANING (at chairside)
  •   - Remove visible debris immediately after use
  •   - Use PPE during cleaning

  • STEP 2: PACKAGING
  •   - Use sterilization pouches or wraps
  •   - Include chemical indicator inside package
  •   - Label with date and contents

  • STEP 3: LOADING
  •   - Do not overload autoclave
  •   - Allow steam circulation
  •   - Place packages on edge, not flat

  • STEP 4: STERILIZATION CYCLE
  •   - Follow manufacturer instructions
  •   - Allow complete cycle including drying

  • STEP 5: STORAGE
  •   - Store in clean, dry area
  •   - Event-related shelf life (not time-related)
  •   - Inspect packages before use

8) STERILIZATION MONITORING

  • PHYSICAL INDICATORS:
  •   - Gauges for temperature, pressure, time
  •   - Printouts from autoclave

  • CHEMICAL INDICATORS:
  •   - External: Indicator tape on packages
  •   - Internal: Indicator strips inside packages
  •   - Class 5 (Integrating): Most reliable, responds to all parameters

  • BIOLOGICAL INDICATORS:
  •   - Spore tests: Geobacillus stearothermophilus (heat-resistant)
  •   - Frequency: Weekly for each sterilizer
  •   - Required for verification of sterilization

9) PPE SEQUENCE

  • DONNING (Putting on - BEFORE patient contact):
  •   1. Perform hand hygiene
  •   2. Put on gown
  •   3. Put on mask/respirator
  •   4. Put on protective eyewear/face shield
  •   5. Put on gloves (over gown cuffs)

  • DOFFING (Removing - AFTER patient contact):
  •   1. Remove gloves (inside-out technique)
  •   2. Remove gown (roll inside-out)
  •   3. Remove eyewear/face shield
  •   4. Remove mask
  •   5. Perform hand hygiene

10) HIGH-YIELD POINTS

  • Hand hygiene is the MOST IMPORTANT infection control measure
  • Standard precautions apply to ALL patients
  • Autoclave uses MOIST heat (steam), not dry heat
  • Biological indicators are the GOLD STANDARD for sterilization monitoring
  • HVE reduces aerosols by 90%+
  • Rubber dam reduces aerosols by 70%+
  • Never recap needles with BOTH hands
  • Sterilization kills ALL organisms including spores
  • Disinfection does NOT kill spores
  • PPE is the LAST line of defense, not the first

11) VIVA ONE-LINERS

  • Chain of infection: Agent -> Reservoir -> Portal of Exit -> Transmission -> Portal of Entry -> Host
  • Standard precautions = Treat all patients as potentially infectious
  • Autoclave: 121C, 15 psi, 15-30 minutes OR 134C, 30 psi, 3-10 minutes
  • Biological indicator: Geobacillus stearothermophilus spores
  • Hand hygiene: Soap and water for 20 seconds OR alcohol rub for 20-30 seconds
  • HVE + Rubber dam = Best aerosol control in dentistry
  • Sterilization kills spores; Disinfection does not
  • PPE: Gloves, Mask, Eyewear, Gown - in that order for donning

 CHAPTER 12: INDICES FOR ORAL DISEASES

GENERAL INDEX CHARACTERISTICS

  • DEFINITION: Numerical score expressing clinical status of oral tissues
  • PURPOSE: Standardized measurement for surveys, monitoring, research
  • IDEAL INDEX: Simple, valid, reliable, sensitive to change, acceptable

PLAQUE INDEX (PII) - Silness & Loe

  • PURPOSE: Measure thickness of plaque at gingival margin
  • TYPE: Reversible, individual assessment
  • TEETH: 12, 11, 26, 16, 36, 31, 46 (buccal and lingual surfaces)

  • SCORING CRITERIA:
  •   0 = No plaque
  •   1 = Thin film at gingival margin (probe visible)
  •   2 = Moderate accumulation (visible to naked eye)
  •   3 = Abundant plaque (thick accumulation)

  • CALCULATION: Total score / Number of surfaces examined
  • INTERPRETATION: 0 = Excellent, 0.1-0.9 = Good, 1.0-1.9 = Fair, 2.0-3.0 = Poor

  • ADVANTAGES: Simple, quick, no disclosing agent needed
  • LIMITATIONS: Only assesses gingival area, not entire tooth

PATIENT HYGIENE PERFORMANCE (PHP) INDEX

  • PURPOSE: Assess oral hygiene status (patient self-performance)
  • TYPE: Reversible, simple
  • TEETH: 12, 11, 31, 32, 36, 37, 46, 45 (buccal and lingual)

  • METHOD: Teeth stained with disclosing agent
  • Each surface divided into 5 sections (scored 0 or 1)

  • SCORING:
  •   0 = No stained debris
  •   1 = Stained debris present

  • CALCULATION: Total stained sections / Total sections examined
  • INTERPRETATION: 0 = Perfect, 0.1-1.7 = Good, 1.8-3.4 = Fair, 3.5-5.0 = Poor

ORAL HYGIENE INDEX (OHI) and OHI-S

  • OHI COMPONENTS:
  •   1. Debris Index (DI): Soft accumulated matter
  •   2. Calculus Index (CI): Mineralized plaque

  • OHI-S (Simplified): Uses 12 surfaces instead of 24
  • TEETH: 12, 11, 26, 16, 36, 31, 46 (buccal and lingual)

  • SCORING (0-3 for each component):
  •   0 = No debris/calculus
  •   1 = Coverage <1/3 of surface
  •   2 = Coverage 1/3 to 2/3 of surface
  •   3 = Coverage >2/3 of surface

  • CALCULATION: OHI-S = (DI-S + CI-S) / 2
  • INTERPRETATION: 0-0.6 = Good, 0.7-1.8 = Fair, 1.9-3.0 = Poor

SULCUS BLEEDING INDEX (SBI) - Muhlemann & Son

  • PURPOSE: Assess gingival inflammation by bleeding tendency
  • TYPE: Reversible, measures inflammation
  • METHOD: Probe gently inserted into sulcus, moved circumferentially

  • SCORING CRITERIA:
  •   0 = Healthy appearance, no bleeding
  •   1 = Healthy appearance, bleeding on probing
  •   2 = Bleeding on probing, change in color, no swelling
  •   3 = Bleeding on probing, change in color, slight swelling
  •   4 = Bleeding on probing, obvious swelling, ulceration
  •   5 = Spontaneous bleeding, marked swelling, ulceration

  • ADVANTAGE: Early indicator of inflammation (before visual changes)

GINGIVAL INDEX (GI) - Loe & Silness

  • PURPOSE: Assess severity of gingival inflammation
  • TYPE: Reversible, individual and group assessment
  • TEETH: Same as PII (12, 11, 26, 16, 36, 31, 46)

  • SCORING CRITERIA:
  •   0 = Normal gingiva
  •   1 = Mild inflammation (slight color change, slight edema)
  •   2 = Moderate inflammation (redness, edema, glazing)
  •   3 = Severe inflammation (marked redness, edema, ulceration, spontaneous bleeding)

  • CALCULATION: Total score / Number of surfaces
  • INTERPRETATION: 0 = Excellent, 0.1-1.0 = Mild, 1.1-2.0 = Moderate, 2.1-3.0 = Severe

CPITN / CPI (Community Periodontal Index of Treatment Needs)

  • PURPOSE: Assess periodontal treatment needs at community level
  • TYPE: WHO recommended for epidemiological surveys
  • TEETH: 10 index teeth (17, 16, 11, 26, 27, 37, 36, 31, 46, 47)
  •   For partial dentition: 6 teeth (16, 11, 26, 36, 31, 46)

  • METHOD: WHO probe (0.5mm ball tip, 3.5mm and 5.5mm markings)
  •   - Light pressure (20-25 grams)
  •   - Walk probe around sulcus

  • SCORING CRITERIA:
  •   0 = Healthy (no bleeding, no calculus, pocket <3.5mm)
  •   1 = Bleeding on probing
  •   2 = Calculus present (visible or detected by probe)
  •   3 = Pocket 4-5mm (probe reaches 3.5mm but not 5.5mm)
  •   4 = Pocket 6mm or more (probe reaches 5.5mm or beyond)
  •   X = Excluded sextant (<2 teeth present)

  • TREATMENT NEEDS:
  •   TN 0: No treatment needed
  •   TN 1: Oral hygiene instruction
  •   TN 2: Scaling and oral hygiene instruction
  •   TN 3: Complex periodontal treatment

DMFT INDEX (Permanent Dentition)

  • PURPOSE: Measure lifetime caries experience
  • TYPE: Irreversible, cumulative
  • COMPONENTS:
  •   D = Decayed teeth (untreated caries)
  •   M = Missing teeth (due to caries)
  •   F = Filled teeth (restored, no decay)

  • CALCULATION: DMFT = D + M + F
  •   Range: 0 to 32 (28 if 3rd molars excluded)

  • INTERPRETATION:
  •   Low caries experience: <5
  •   Moderate: 5-10
  •   High: >10

  • LIMITATIONS:
  •   - Does not measure severity (1 surface = whole tooth)
  •   - Cannot distinguish between past and present disease
  •   - M component may be overestimated (missing for other reasons)

DMFS INDEX (Permanent Dentition)

  • PURPOSE: More sensitive measure of caries experience (tooth surface level)
  • TYPE: Irreversible, cumulative
  • SURFACES PER TOOTH:
  •   Anteriors: 4 surfaces (mesial, distal, labial, lingual)
  •   Posteriors: 5 surfaces (+ occlusal)
  •   Total possible: 128 surfaces (or 88 if 3rd molars excluded)

  • CALCULATION: DMFS = D surfaces + M surfaces + F surfaces
  •   Missing tooth = all surfaces missing
  •   Filled tooth with recurrent decay = F + D surfaces

  • ADVANTAGE: More sensitive to change than DMFT
  • DISADVANTAGE: More time-consuming to record

SIC INDEX (Significant Caries Index)

  • PURPOSE: Identify high-risk groups within population
  • METHOD:
  •   1. Calculate DMFT for entire population
  •   2. Identify one-third with HIGHEST DMFT
  •   3. Calculate mean DMFT of this top third

  • SIGNIFICANCE:
  •   - Identifies 'polarization' of caries
  •   - Helps target high-risk populations
  •   - More meaningful than mean DMFT alone

Caries Indices for Primary Dentition

  • dmft (lowercase): For primary teeth
  •   d = decayed primary teeth
  •   m = missing primary teeth (due to caries)
  •   f = filled primary teeth

  • dmfs: Surface-level measure for primary teeth
  •   20 teeth x 5 surfaces = 100 possible surfaces

  • NOTE: 'e' (extracted) sometimes used instead of 'm' for children
  • def index: d + e + f

DEAN'S FLUOROSIS INDEX

  • PURPOSE: Assess severity of dental fluorosis
  • TYPE: Irreversible, community assessment

  • SCORING (0-6 scale):
  •   0 = Normal: Smooth, glossy, translucent enamel
  •   0.5 = Questionable: Small opaque areas, <25% of surface
  •   1 = Very Mild: Opaque areas, 25-50% of surface
  •   2 = Mild: Opaque areas, >50% of surface
  •   3 = Moderate: All surfaces affected, brown stains, slight pitting
  •   4 = Severe: All surfaces affected, marked pitting, brown stains

  • COMMUNITY ASSESSMENT: Record TWO most severe teeth

COMMUNITY FLUOROSIS INDEX (CFI)

  • PURPOSE: Measure fluorosis at community level
  • FORMULA: CFI = Sum of (Score x Number of individuals with that score) / Total number examined

  • INTERPRETATION:
  •   0.0-0.4 = Negative (no public health concern)
  •   0.4-0.6 = Borderline
  •   0.6-1.0 = Slight (public health concern)
  •   1.0-2.0 = Mild
  •   2.0-3.0 = Moderate
  •   3.0-4.0 = Marked

  • PUBLIC HEALTH SIGNIFICANCE:
  •   CFI >0.6 indicates need for defluoridation

INDEX COMPARISON TABLE

  • INDEX          | TYPE        | REVERSIBLE | USE
  • ---------------|-------------|------------|------------------------
  • PII            | Plaque      | Yes        | Individual assessment
  • PHP            | Plaque      | Yes        | Patient self-evaluation
  • OHI-S          | Hygiene     | Yes        | Community surveys
  • GI             | Gingival    | Yes        | Inflammation severity
  • SBI            | Gingival    | Yes        | Early inflammation
  • CPITN/CPI      | Periodontal | Yes        | Treatment needs (WHO)
  • DMFT           | Caries      | No         | Lifetime experience
  • DMFS           | Caries      | No         | Sensitive caries measure
  • Dean's         | Fluorosis   | No         | Severity assessment
  • CFI            | Fluorosis   | No         | Community fluorosis

HIGH-YIELD INDEX POINTS

  • DMFT is CUMULATIVE and IRREVERSIBLE
  • DMFS is more SENSITIVE than DMFT (surface-level)
  • CPITN uses WHO probe with 3.5mm and 5.5mm markings
  • CPITN scores 0-4; Treatment Needs 0-3
  • GI and PII use same teeth (12, 11, 26, 16, 36, 31, 46)
  • SBI measures bleeding on probing (early inflammation)
  • Dean's Index: 0=Normal, 4=Severe fluorosis
  • CFI >0.6 indicates public health concern for fluorosis
  • SIC Index identifies high-risk one-third of population

 SECTION E: PREVENTIVE DENTISTRY

1) LEVELS OF PREVENTION

  • PRIMORDIAL PREVENTION:
  •   - Prevent risk factors from emerging
  •   - Target: General population, especially children
  •   - Dental Examples: National dietary guidelines, breastfeeding promotion

  • PRIMARY PREVENTION:
  •   - Prevent disease onset in susceptible individuals
  •   - Target: Healthy individuals
  •   - Dental Examples: Fluoride, sealants, oral hygiene instruction

  • SECONDARY PREVENTION:
  •   - Early detection and prompt treatment
  •   - Target: Early disease stages
  •   - Dental Examples: Screening, early caries detection, minimal intervention

  • TERTIARY PREVENTION:
  •   - Restore function, prevent complications
  •   - Target: Established disease
  •   - Dental Examples: Restorations, RCT, extractions, prosthetics

2) PLAQUE CONTROL

Mechanical Plaque Control

  • TOOTHBRUSHING:
  •   - Most common and effective mechanical method
  •   - Bass method: Bristles at 45-degree angle to gingiva, vibrate
  •   - Modified Bass: Add sweeping motion occlusally
  •   - Frequency: Twice daily for 2 minutes
  •   - Brush replacement: Every 3 months or when frayed

  • INTERDENTAL AIDS:
  •   - Dental floss: Type based on contact tightness
  •   - Interdental brushes: For larger spaces, bridges
  •   - Wooden sticks: Triangular, for posterior areas
  •   - Oral irrigators: Adjunct, not substitute

Chemical Plaque Control

  • CHLORHEXIDINE (0.12-0.2%):
  •   - Gold standard antimicrobial mouthwash
  •   - Mechanism: Disrupts bacterial cell membrane
  •   - Side effects: Staining, altered taste, calculus formation
  •   - Use: Short-term (1-2 weeks), post-surgical

  • ESSENTIAL OILS (Listerine):
  •   - Thymol, eucalyptol, menthol, methyl salicylate
  •   - Less staining than chlorhexidine
  •   - Can be used long-term

  • OTHER AGENTS:
  •   - Triclosan: Antimicrobial in toothpaste
  •   - Cetylpyridinium chloride (CPC): Mild antiseptic
  •   - Fluoride: Primarily anti-caries, mild plaque effect

Disclosing Agents

  • PURPOSE: Visualize plaque for patient education and self-assessment
  • TYPES: Erythrosine (red), fluorescein (fluorescent under UV)
  • USE:
  •   - Apply after rinsing, before brushing
  •   - Shows areas missed during brushing
  •   - Useful for teaching proper technique
  •   - Used in PHP index assessment

3) CARIES ACTIVITY TESTS

  • DEFINITION: Laboratory/biochemical tests to assess caries risk

  • TYPES:
  •   1. SNYDER TEST: Measures acid production by salivary bacteria
  •      - Color change indicates acid production

  •   2. ALBANS TEST: Similar to Snyder test

  •   3. STRIP MUTANS: Detects S.mutans levels in saliva

  •   4. SALIVARY FLOW RATE: Measures unstimulated and stimulated flow
  •      - Low flow = increased caries risk

  •   5. SALIVARY BUFFERING CAPACITY: Ability to neutralize acid

  • LIMITATIONS:
  •   - Not widely used in clinical practice
  •   - Do not predict caries with high accuracy
  •   - Expensive and time-consuming

4) PIT AND FISSURE SEALANTS

Definition and Purpose

  • DEFINITION: Material placed in pits and fissures to prevent caries
  • PURPOSE: Physical barrier preventing bacterial colonization
  • MECHANISM: Seals off retentive areas where toothbrush cannot reach

Indications

  • Deep, retentive pits and fissures
  • History of caries in primary teeth (high risk)
  • Early signs of caries in fissures (non-cavitated)
  • Orthodontic patients with appliances
  • Children with special needs
  • Post-orthodontic treatment for permanent molars

Contraindications

  • Well-coalesced, self-cleansing fissures
  • Obvious caries requiring restoration
  • Teeth that have been erupted >4 years (unless high risk)
  • Inability to maintain isolation
  • Patient/parent unwilling to accept treatment

Clinical Procedure

  • STEP 1: Clean tooth surface (pumice slurry)
  • STEP 2: Isolate and maintain dryness (rubber dam preferred)
  • STEP 3: Etch with 35-38% phosphoric acid (20-30 seconds)
  • STEP 4: Rinse thoroughly (20-30 seconds)
  • STEP 5: Dry thoroughly (chalky white appearance)
  • STEP 6: Apply sealant material
  • STEP 7: Light cure (20-40 seconds depending on material)
  • STEP 8: Check occlusion and adjust if needed

Types of Sealant Materials

  • RESIN-BASED (BIS-GMA):
  •   - Most common type
  •   - Requires acid etching
  •   - Types: Filled vs Unfilled, Clear vs Tinted
  •   - Cure: Self-cure or Light-cure

  • GLASS IONOMER:
  •   - Chemical adhesion (no etching needed)
  •   - Fluoride release
  •   - Lower retention than resin
  •   - Use: Moisture control difficult, transitional sealant

Reasons for Sealant Failure

  • Moisture contamination during placement
  • Inadequate etching or rinsing
  • Insufficient curing time
  • Air bubbles in material
  • Occlusal interference
  • Saliva contamination before polymerization

5) CARIES VACCINE

  • CONCEPT: Immunization against S.mutans to prevent colonization

  • TARGETS:
  •   - Antigens: Glucosyltransferase (GTF), Antigen I/II
  •   - Mechanism: Prevent bacterial adhesion to tooth surface

  • CHALLENGES:
  •   - S.mutans is part of normal flora (ethical concerns)
  •   - Multiple bacteria involved in caries (not just S.mutans)
  •   - Need for booster immunizations
  •   - Cost-effectiveness concerns

  • CURRENT STATUS:
  •   - Research ongoing, no commercial vaccine available
  •   - Not routine in dental practice

6) MINIMAL INTERVENTION DENTISTRY (MID)

Principles

  • 1. Identify and modify caries risk factors
  • 2. Early detection of non-cavitated lesions
  • 3. Remineralization of early lesions
  • 4. Minimal tissue removal when restoration needed
  • 5. Repair rather than replace restorations
  • 6. Use of long-lasting restorative materials

Techniques

  • REMINERALIZATION:
  •   - Fluoride: Topical applications, high-fluoride toothpaste
  •   - CPP-ACP (Casein phosphopeptide-amorphous calcium phosphate): Recaldent
  •   - Xylitol: Reduces S.mutans levels

  • MINIMAL CAVITY PREPARATION:
  •   - Air abrasion
  •   - Chemomechanical caries removal (Carisolv)
  •   - Lasers

  • ADHESIVE RESTORATIONS:
  •   - Preserve tooth structure
  •   - Seal margins to prevent microleakage

7) ATRAUMATIC RESTORATIVE TREATMENT (ART)

Concept

  • DEFINITION: Minimal intervention approach using hand instruments only
  • DEVELOPED BY: WHO for developing countries
  • PRINCIPLE: Remove soft, demineralized tissue; restore with glass ionomer

Indications

  • Small to medium carious lesions
  • No pulp involvement
  • Areas without electricity (no dental equipment)
  • Community outreach programs
  • Children and anxious patients
  • Elderly and special needs patients

Procedure

  • STEP 1: Isolate with cotton rolls
  • STEP 2: Remove soft caries with hand excavators
  • STEP 3: Clean cavity with wet cotton pellet
  • STEP 4: Condition with polyacrylic acid (if needed)
  • STEP 5: Mix and place high-viscosity glass ionomer
  • STEP 6: Compress with finger pressure (apron technique)
  • STEP 7: Remove excess, check occlusion
  • STEP 8: Apply varnish/petroleum jelly for protection

Advantages and Disadvantages

  • ADVANTAGES:
  •   - No expensive equipment needed
  •   - No electricity required
  •   - Less traumatic (no drill)
  •   - Chemical adhesion (no retention form needed)
  •   - Fluoride release

  • DISADVANTAGES:
  •   - Limited to small/medium lesions
  •   - Technique sensitive
  •   - Moisture control critical
  •   - Lower survival rate than conventional restorations
  •   - Not suitable for all lesions

8) FLUORIDES IN CARIES PREVENTION

Natural Occurrence and History

  • Naturally found in water, soil, rocks, some foods
  • Optimal water fluoride: 0.7-1.2 ppm (temperature dependent)
  • HISTORY:
  •   - 1901: Frederick McKay observed 'Colorado brown stain'
  •   - 1931: Fluoride identified as cause
  •   - 1945: Grand Rapids - first water fluoridation trial
  •   - Reduction in caries: 40-60%

Systemic vs Topical Fluoride

  • SYSTEMIC FLUORIDE:
  •   - Ingested, systemic distribution
  •   - Benefits: Pre-eruptive (incorporation into developing enamel)
  •   - Methods: Water fluoridation, dietary supplements
  •   - Caution: Risk of fluorosis during tooth development

  • TOPICAL FLUORIDE:
  •   - Applied to erupted teeth
  •   - Benefits: Post-eruptive (surface effects)
  •   - Methods: Toothpaste, mouthwash, varnish, gel, foam
  •   - No fluorosis risk (except young children who swallow)

Mechanism of Action

  • 1. INHIBITION OF DEMINERALIZATION:
  •    - Fluoride incorporates into enamel, makes it more acid-resistant

  • 2. ENHANCEMENT OF REMINERALIZATION:
  •    - Fluoride attracts calcium and phosphate to repair early lesions

  • 3. INHIBITION OF BACTERIAL ENZYMES:
  •    - Interferes with bacterial acid production

  • 4. INCORPORATION INTO ENAMEL (pre-eruptive):
  •    - Forms fluorapatite (more acid-resistant than hydroxyapatite)

Fluoride Toxicity

  • ACUTE TOXICITY:
  •   - Dose: >5 mg/kg body weight (probable toxic dose)
  •   - Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea
  •   - Severe: Hypocalcemia, cardiac arrhythmia, death
  •   - TREATMENT: Induce vomiting, give calcium (milk, calcium gluconate)

  • CHRONIC TOXICITY:
  •   - Manifestation: Dental fluorosis (enamel hypomineralization)
  •   - Skeletal fluorosis (very high intake over long period)

  • PREVENTION:
  •   - Supervise children during tooth brushing
  •   - Pea-sized amount of toothpaste for children <6 years
  •   - Store fluoride products out of reach of children

Fluoride Delivery Methods

  • COMMUNITY LEVEL:
  •   - Water fluoridation (most cost-effective)
  •   - Salt fluoridation (alternative where water not feasible)
  •   - Milk fluoridation (school programs)

  • PROFESSIONAL APPLICATION:
  •   - Fluoride varnish (22,600 ppm F): 2-4 times/year
  •   - Fluoride gel/foam (12,300 ppm F): Tray application
  •   - Silver diamine fluoride (SDF): Arrests cavitated caries

  • SELF-APPLIED:
  •   - Fluoride toothpaste (1000-1500 ppm F): Twice daily
  •   - Fluoride mouthwash (225-900 ppm F): Daily/weekly
  •   - High-fluoride toothpaste (5000 ppm F): Prescription for high risk

 SECTION F: HEALTH STATISTICS

APPLIED BIOSTATISTICS & RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

1) BASIC TERMINOLOGIES

  • DATA: Collection of observations or measurements
  • VARIABLE: Characteristic that can vary (age, DMFT, blood pressure)
  • POPULATION: Entire group of interest (all BDS students in India)
  • SAMPLE: Subset of population selected for study
  • PARAMETER: Numerical characteristic of population (mean DMFT of all Indians)
  • STATISTIC: Numerical characteristic of sample (mean DMFT of study participants)

2) TYPES OF VARIABLES

  • QUALITATIVE (Categorical):
  •   - Nominal: Categories without order (gender, blood group, yes/no)
  •   - Ordinal: Categories with order (mild/moderate/severe, GI scores)

  • QUANTITATIVE (Numerical):
  •   - Discrete: Whole numbers only (number of teeth, DMFT score)
  •   - Continuous: Any value in range (age, salivary flow rate, pocket depth)

3) SCALES OF MEASUREMENT

  • NOMINAL SCALE:
  •   - Categories, no order
  •   - Dental Example: Gender (M/F), Caries present (Yes/No)

  • ORDINAL SCALE:
  •   - Categories with order/rank
  •   - Dental Example: GI scores (0-3), Dean's fluorosis index
  •   - Intervals not equal

  • INTERVAL SCALE:
  •   - Equal intervals, no true zero
  •   - Dental Example: Temperature (Celsius), Calendar dates

  • RATIO SCALE:
  •   - Equal intervals, true zero exists
  •   - Dental Example: DMFT, age, pocket depth, income
  •   - Can calculate ratios (20 years is twice 10 years)

4) METHODS OF DATA COLLECTION

  • SURVEY: Questionnaires, interviews (cross-sectional data)
  • CLINICAL EXAMINATION: Direct observation, measurements (DMFT, CPI)
  • RECORDS REVIEW: Hospital records, dental charts
  • LABORATORY TESTS: Salivary analysis, microbiological tests

5) DATA PRESENTATION

Tables

  • Frequency distribution table
  • Contingency table (2x2 for association studies)
  • Components: Title, Row/Column headings, Footnotes, Source

Charts and Graphs

  • BAR CHART: For categorical data (comparison between groups)
  •   Dental Example: DMFT by age group

  • HISTOGRAM: For continuous data (frequency distribution)
  •   Dental Example: Distribution of pocket depths
  •   No gaps between bars (continuous data)

  • PIE CHART: Proportions of whole (percentages)
  •   Dental Example: Treatment needs distribution (TN0, TN1, TN2, TN3)

  • LINE GRAPH: Trends over time
  •   Dental Example: Caries prevalence over 10 years

  • SCATTER PLOT: Relationship between two continuous variables
  •   Dental Example: Sugar intake vs DMFT

6) MEASURES OF CENTRAL TENDENCY

Mean (Average)

  • Formula: Sum of all values / Number of values
  • Symbol: x-bar (sample), mu (population)
  • USE: Symmetric distribution, no extreme outliers
  • DENTAL EXAMPLE: Mean DMFT of sample = 3.5
  • LIMITATION: Affected by extreme values (outliers)

Median

  • DEFINITION: Middle value when data arranged in order
  • USE: Skewed distribution, ordinal data, with outliers
  • DENTAL EXAMPLE: Median pocket depth (less affected by extreme values)
  • ADVANTAGE: Not affected by extreme values

Mode

  • DEFINITION: Most frequently occurring value
  • USE: Categorical data, identifying common category
  • DENTAL EXAMPLE: Most common CPI score in community
  • LIMITATION: May have multiple modes or no mode

When to Use Each

  • SYMMETRIC DISTRIBUTION: Mean = Median = Mode
  • SKEWED RIGHT (positive): Mean > Median (outliers on right)
  • SKEWED LEFT (negative): Mean < Median (outliers on left)
  • For skewed data: Use MEDIAN

7) MEASURES OF DISPERSION

Range

  • Formula: Maximum value - Minimum value
  • LIMITATION: Only uses two values, affected by outliers
  • DENTAL EXAMPLE: DMFT range = 0-15

Standard Deviation (SD)

  • DEFINITION: Average distance of values from mean
  • Formula: Square root of variance
  • INTERPRETATION:
  •   - Small SD = Data clustered closely around mean
  •   - Large SD = Data spread out
  • DENTAL EXAMPLE: Mean DMFT = 3.5, SD = 1.2

Variance

  • DEFINITION: Average of squared deviations from mean
  • Formula: SD squared
  • Units: Squared units (difficult to interpret)

Coefficient of Variation (CV)

  • Formula: (SD / Mean) x 100
  • USE: Compare variability between different scales
  • DENTAL EXAMPLE: Compare variability of DMFT vs pocket depth
  • INTERPRETATION: CV <15% = Homogeneous, CV >30% = Heterogeneous

8) NORMAL DISTRIBUTION

  • DEFINITION: Bell-shaped, symmetric distribution
  • CHARACTERISTICS:
  •   - Mean = Median = Mode
  •   - Symmetric around mean
  •   - Tails extend to infinity

  • 68-95-99.7 RULE (Empirical Rule):
  •   - 68% of data within Mean +/- 1 SD
  •   - 95% of data within Mean +/- 2 SD
  •   - 99.7% of data within Mean +/- 3 SD

  • DENTAL EXAMPLE:
  •   Mean DMFT = 3.0, SD = 1.0
  •   68% of population has DMFT between 2.0 and 4.0
  •   95% of population has DMFT between 1.0 and 5.0

9) CONFIDENCE INTERVAL

  • DEFINITION: Range of values likely to contain true population parameter
  • INTERPRETATION: 95% CI means 95% confident true value lies within range

  • 95% CI FORMULA: Mean +/- (1.96 x Standard Error)
  • Standard Error = SD / Square root of n

  • DENTAL EXAMPLE:
  •   Sample mean DMFT = 3.5, SD = 1.2, n = 100
  •   SE = 1.2 / 10 = 0.12
  •   95% CI = 3.5 +/- (1.96 x 0.12) = 3.5 +/- 0.24 = (3.26, 3.74)
  •   Interpretation: We are 95% confident true population mean is between 3.26 and 3.74

10) PROBABILITY

  • DEFINITION: Likelihood of an event occurring (0 to 1, or 0% to 100%)
  • P = 0: Impossible event
  • P = 1: Certain event
  • P = 0.5: Equally likely to occur or not occur

  • DENTAL EXAMPLE:
  •   Probability of caries in high-sugar consumers = 0.7 (70%)

11) TESTS OF SIGNIFICANCE

p-value

  • DEFINITION: Probability of obtaining observed results if null hypothesis is true
  • INTERPRETATION:
  •   - p < 0.05: Statistically significant (reject null hypothesis)
  •   - p < 0.01: Highly significant
  •   - p < 0.001: Very highly significant
  •   - p > 0.05: Not statistically significant (fail to reject null)

  • COMMON MISTAKE: p-value does NOT measure probability that hypothesis is true

t-test

  • USE: Compare means of TWO groups (continuous data)
  • TYPES:
  •   - Independent t-test: Two unrelated groups
  •   - Paired t-test: Same group measured twice (before/after)

  • DENTAL EXAMPLES:
  •   - Independent: Compare mean DMFT between boys and girls
  •   - Paired: Compare plaque scores before and after brushing instruction

Chi-square test

  • USE: Test association between TWO categorical variables
  • REQUIREMENT: Expected frequency in each cell >5

  • DENTAL EXAMPLES:
  •   - Association between gender and caries experience (Yes/No)
  •   - Association between smoking and periodontal disease
  •   - Association between fluoride exposure and fluorosis

  • INTERPRETATION:
  •   - Significant p-value: Association exists between variables
  •   - Non-significant p-value: No association detected

12) RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

Components of Research

  • AIM/OBJECTIVE: What the study intends to achieve
  • HYPOTHESIS: Testable statement predicting relationship
  •   - Null hypothesis (H0): No relationship exists
  •   - Alternative hypothesis (H1): Relationship exists
  • VARIABLES:
  •   - Independent: Exposure/intervention (predictor)
  •   - Dependent: Outcome (response)
  •   - Confounding: Affects both independent and dependent

Types of Research

  • DESCRIPTIVE: Describe patterns (Person, Place, Time)
  •   Dental Example: Prevalence of malocclusion in schoolchildren

  • ANALYTICAL: Test hypotheses, examine relationships
  •   - Observational: Case-control, Cohort, Cross-sectional
  •   Dental Example: Association between sugar and caries

  • EXPERIMENTAL: Intervention with randomization
  •   - RCT: Gold standard
  •   Dental Example: Effect of fluoride varnish on caries reduction

Sampling Methods

  • SIMPLE RANDOM SAMPLING: Every member has equal chance
  •   Method: Lottery, random number tables

  • SYSTEMATIC SAMPLING: Select every kth individual
  •   Method: Select every 10th patient from list

  • STRATIFIED SAMPLING: Divide population into strata, sample from each
  •   Dental Example: Sample from each age group separately
  •   Ensures representation of subgroups

  • CLUSTER SAMPLING: Select groups (clusters), sample all within
  •   Dental Example: Select schools, examine all children in selected schools
  •   Useful when population dispersed

Sample Size

  • FACTORS AFFECTING SAMPLE SIZE:
  •   - Effect size (larger effect = smaller sample needed)
  •   - Significance level (alpha, usually 0.05)
  •   - Power (1-beta, usually 0.80 or 80%)
  •   - Variability in population (more variable = larger sample)

  • CONSEQUENCES:
  •   - Too small: Study underpowered, may miss true effect
  •   - Too large: Wasteful, unethical to expose more than needed

Bias and Control

  • SELECTION BIAS: Systematic differences between groups
  •   Control: Randomization, inclusion/exclusion criteria

  • INFORMATION BIAS: Errors in measurement/data collection
  •   Types: Observer bias, Recall bias
  •   Control: Blinding, standardized protocols

  • CONFOUNDING: Third variable affects exposure-outcome relationship
  •   Control: Randomization, Matching, Stratification, Multivariate analysis
